01 — Core Concepts

What is
Retrieval-Augmented Generation?

LLMs are powerful — but they don't know what happened last week, can't read your internal documents, and will confidently hallucinate when asked about things outside their training. RAG fixes that by retrieving relevant context at query time and grounding every response in your actual data.

12 min read 3 interactive diagrams Scroll to explore
01 — The Problem

LLMs are frozen in time

When a language model is trained, its knowledge is sealed at a cutoff date. Ask it about your internal documentation, recent events, or proprietary data and one of two things happens: it says "I don't know" — or, more dangerously, it makes something up with complete confidence.

This isn't a bug. It's how all language models work. Knowledge lives entirely in the model's weights, baked in during training. There's no live connection to the outside world.

🗓
Knowledge cutoff
Unaware of anything after training data ended. Events, new docs, updated policies — invisible.
🔒
No private data
Can't access your internal documents, wikis, support tickets, databases, or anything not in training.
👻
Hallucination
Generates plausible-sounding falsehoods when it doesn't know the answer. Confident and wrong.

02 — The Solution

Retrieve context, then generate

RAG solves the problem by finding relevant information from your corpus at query time and injecting it into the model's prompt before generation. The model doesn't need to memorise your documents — it reads them on demand, right before answering.

Think of it like the difference between asking someone to recall facts from memory versus handing them the relevant source material and asking them to summarise it. The answer is grounded, verifiable, and current.

The key insight: RAG separates where knowledge is stored (your document corpus) from where language generation happens (the LLM). Update one independently of the other — no retraining needed.

03 — How It Works

Three steps. Every time.

Every RAG system follows the same fundamental loop. The sophistication lives in how each step is implemented — and that's what the rest of this guide covers.

STEP 01
🔍
Retrieve
Search the document corpus for chunks most semantically or lexically similar to the user's query.
→
STEP 02
📎
Augment
Inject retrieved chunks into the model's prompt alongside the original query. Give the LLM the raw material it needs.
→
STEP 03
✦
Generate
The LLM generates a response grounded in retrieved context — verifiable, current, and not hallucinated from memory.
04 — Critical Distinction

Indexing is offline. Retrieval is live.

RAG has two distinct phases that happen at completely different times — and this is where most beginners get confused.

Indexing happens once, in the background. Documents are split into chunks, each chunk is embedded into a vector, and all vectors are stored in a vector database. This can take minutes or hours — it doesn't matter.

Retrieval happens at query time, on every request. The query is embedded, the vector store is searched, and top-K chunks are returned in under 100ms.

Rule to remember: Chunking is always an offline step. It never runs during a live query. If you see a RAG diagram showing "Chunking" in the live query path — it's architecturally wrong.
